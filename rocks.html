Rocks 101

Three kinds of rocks, one little planet.

Every stone you've ever picked up is one of three things: born of fire, built from layers, or remade by heat & pressure. Here's the pocket guide.

01The Three Types

Tap a card to dig in.

How it forms

Molten rock (magma underground, lava above) cools and crystallizes. Cool slowly deep inside the Earth and you get big crystals; cool fast at the surface and you get tiny ones — or no crystals at all.

Key features

Interlocking crystals, no layering, often very hard. May contain frozen-in gas bubbles (vesicles) if it cooled fast.

Common examples

  • GraniteSlow-cooled deep underground. Speckled pink/gray, the bones of mountain ranges.
  • BasaltFast-cooled lava. Dark, fine-grained — covers most of the ocean floor.
  • ObsidianLava cooled so fast it never crystallized. Volcanic glass — sharp edges.
  • PumiceFrothy, gas-filled lava. Light enough to float on water.

Where you'll find it

Volcanic regions, mountain cores, mid-ocean ridges. In Utah: the lava flows of Snow Canyon and the granite of Little Cottonwood Canyon.

How it forms

Wind, water, and ice break older rocks into bits. Those sediments settle, pile up, get buried, and slowly cement into stone. Sometimes the rock precipitates straight out of seawater, or is built from the shells of tiny sea creatures.

Key features

Visible layers (strata), often softer than other rock types. The only kind that can carry fossils. May fizz with vinegar (limestone), split into sheets (shale), or feel gritty (sandstone).

Common examples

  • SandstoneCemented sand grains. The orange cliffs of Zion and Arches — hello, southern Utah.
  • LimestoneOften built from ancient shells & coral. Fizzes when you drip vinegar on it.
  • ShaleCompacted mud. Splits into thin flakes; the original source of most fossil fuels.
  • ConglomerateRounded pebbles glued together — nature's concrete.

Where you'll find it

Riverbeds, deserts, ancient seabeds, basically anywhere flat. In Utah: nearly every red, orange, or tan cliff you've ever photographed.

How it forms

An existing rock (igneous or sedimentary) gets buried deep, squeezed, and heated — but doesn't quite melt. Its minerals re-grow, line up, or swap into new ones. The result is the same chemistry, reorganized.

Key features

Often banded or foliated (stripes from minerals lining up under pressure). Usually denser and harder than the parent rock. Sometimes sparkly with mica or shot through with veins.

Common examples

  • MarbleLimestone, recooked. Smooth, sometimes swirly — the stuff of statues.
  • SlateShale, squeezed. Splits into perfect flat plates — old-school chalkboards and roof tiles.
  • GneissGranite or shale, cranked up. Strongly banded light/dark stripes (pronounced "nice").
  • SchistMica-rich and sparkly in the sun. Common in old mountain belts.

Where you'll find it

The cores of mountain ranges, deep continental crust, old collision zones. In Utah: parts of the Wasatch and the Uinta Range expose ancient metamorphic rock.
